For decades, Native languages was shrouded in secrecy, mostly passed down from elders to the younger generations. As the world modernized and tribal communities shrunk, these languages faced the prospect of extinction. Then along came Mosiah Bluecloud.

Bluecloud is a member of the Kickapoo Tribe of Oklahoma, and is one of the few who advocate for the revitalization of tribal languages in the state. He specializes in teaching the Sauk, Shawnee and Kickapoo languages ranging from high school to college classes, and holds bachelor’s and master’s degrees in Linguistics.


He attributes his motivation for teaching to his experience learning languages growing up.

“Um, you know, I had to work at things extra hard and I always had top grades in high school and college. And even now, I work really hard. It’s not through some kind of gifted high IQ ,” he said.

However, Bluecloud says that some students fall through the cracks and get left behind if they don’t perform academically well. His strategy is to structure his teaching so that both strong and weak students benefit.

“There is a Goldilocks area of instruction where you can teach to your most – the weakest links, what we call the barometer students – you can teach to your barometer students. And if you teach correctly, you can keep the top students engaged as well. And you can actually – a good teacher can facilitate peer to peer learning between the top students and the weakest links.”

Bluecloud says that it’s rare that languages are correctly taught. Because of this, he works with language teachers to improve the standard of Native language education.

“I go into the Cheyenne-Arapaho community, Sauk community, Shawnee community and the Kickapoo community and um, I work with the people that I teach, I tell them about the barometer students and how to teach with compassion and attentive. And I feel like I’m making a difference with that.”

Throughout his linguistics career, he has faced multiple setbacks. Because of the secretive nature of Native languages, he drew criticism from members of his own tribe as a Kickapoo language teacher.

“When I started teaching the language to fellow tribal members, there were two individuals that, they forged four hundred signatures, they forged a cease and desist letter and they told me that I wasn’t allowed to teach. It looked official, and so I was like wow, four hundred of my own people. And I looked on the list and there were my family members on there.”



Bluecloud said that he was stricken by the letter.

“I thought, I’m not going to go against my own family, my own tribe, so I guess I won’t teach anymore. I stopped and I went into my little hole of shame”

After a thorough investigation by the Kickapoo business committee, the letter was found to be a forgery. There was no grounds behind it. Bluecloud said that the Kickapoo community rallied around him.

“They were like ‘Mosiah we need you, we love you. We need you to do this work, we need you to continue. Those two individuals, (though) very loud individuals, do not represent the tribe.’ I was like ‘you’re right’. I was grateful for everybody, finding that out and getting me back.”

Bluecloud said that the community support inspired him to build up the Kickapoo language department.

“I fixed the Kickapoo language department and we started from materials development, passing out dictionaries and grammar and everything for kids, and audio CDs and DVDs, just pumping out everything they could and giving it out to the communities.”

Another recurring obstacle that stood in the way of Bluecloud’s work was the lack of funding.

“The bigger tribes are the ones that get the attention, and they’re also the ones that get the money. So you know, I applied for grants when I was director, and um it was just me, one guy going against the Chickasaws, the Cherokees, the Creeks that have a team of professional grant writers.”

Competing with bigger tribes that employed professional grant writers meant that as the Kickapoo tribe’s language director, Bluecloud was not able to get much funding.

“They got a team of professional grant writers getting paid big bucks to write these grants, and then little me (laughs) that’s just a teacher. I’m just a language teacher doing my best trying to put a grant together.”

Instead, the Kickapoo language department worked with ‘wrap funds’, or leftover money from what the tribe’s casinos used. Bluecloud pointed out that the tribe’s language department aspires to use the Master-Apprentice language learning method to teach language. The method pairs an elder speaker of the language with a language learner.

“The more speakers you get in it, the faster the learning process. (sighs) It’s expensive though. It’s expensive. And all the big tribes are doing it. The Chickasaws, the Cherokees, everyone is doing (sic) Master-Apprentice. I wanted my tribe to do it, but we can’t do it off of wrap funding. So yeah that’s my biggest obstacle, it’s just funding.”

Bluecloud also mentioned that the COVID-19 pandemic had affected Native communities and language acquisition. For about a year, tribe members that interacted each other every day had to stay apart, which was hard on the tight-knit communities.

However, there was a positive side to things. Bluecloud said that the rapid normalization of online learning meant that distance language learners and tribal members could once again be part of the community.

“Tribal members that were in California and Washington and Florida and miles away from Oklahoma, they were brought into the fold of language learning. Before, nobody was trying to teach things online, really. There was no online classes. If you wanted to learn a language, you come to the community, you visit the elders, you find your place and come to ceremonies.”

“When the online classes were the only way, we’re seeing all of these new faces, we’re meeting all of these new people, hearing all their stories and how their families ended up so far away, and how they developed roots. They are where they’re at but they’re still Kickapoo, they’re still Shawnee and still Sauk.”

“They still want to learn, they still want to be connected. And we’ve been reconnecting them. And getting reconnected to them and that’s a huge plus.”



Bluecloud said that in order to prevent language extinction, tribes should focus on community building. He mentioned that unlike the past, the tribes do not live in small villages any more where generations of tribe members used to interact with each other regularly.

“When you’ve got people spread out in different towns surrounded by dominant languages kids just don’t see the use in learning languages and that’s why they’ve been disappearing.”

“Right now, a lot of tribes they build up their housing complexes and they put elders in there, which is great. We need to take care of our elders but we need to start building apartment complexes as well. For the younger families.”

Bluecloud suggested that the tribes could focus their efforts on intergenerational housing solutions.

“Right now, a lot of tribes they build up their housing complexes and they put elders in there, which is great. We need to take care of our elders but we need to start building apartment complexes as well. For the younger families.”

“We need to get kids placed next to their elders. We have to make that connection between elders and the children.”
